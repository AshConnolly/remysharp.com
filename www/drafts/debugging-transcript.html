Debugging from my perspective, caveat, no, I don't use frameworks.

Debugging big frameworks isn't something I do, because I don't need them. I don't personally believe they'll be around in the next 5-10 years, and I have code that already exists. This doesn't mean you shouldn't use frameworks, it just means that I haven't, because I don't have those kinds of problems to solve.

Related to the complexity of debugging. Debugging is an art. There's no silver bullet, all the tools in the world, but it boils down to experience, having seen lots of bugs, having created lots of bugs, being thrown into the deep end, and having to solve the problems.

The more times you solve those problems, the better equipped you are to solve new problems.

For example, if you take any decent CSS developer, give them a layout, and ask why doesn't this thing float correctly, they'll tell you to add zoom: 1. It's unlikely they're throwing a random guess at you, but in fact what they're doing is taking all their experience having seen that problem before in it's different shapes and forms, and being able to very quickly see the path debugging and solving this particular problem ... is to add zoom: 1 - all in one step (in their head). And that comes from experience, solving the problem over and over.

The same is true with JS or any other language.

Debugging on the web has lots of different forms: HTML, CSS, JS. JS debuggging in node, in the browser, for different browsers.

I've met a lot of people over the years that have said "wouldn't this be a lot easier if tehre was just one single browser?". Sure, of course it would be easier (ignoring the competitive benefits of having multiple browsers, innovation, etc), but sure, debugging would be easier, but we'd just be writing code. Punching the keys.

The web is fluid. And that's what makes this work interesting and challenging.
